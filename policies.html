A Note about the philosophy of this course:


Printmaking classes in particular can tend to focus on the basic technical skills (since there are so many!) and fail to encourage students to develop their own unique interests. To study printmaking should not be about completing assignments and learning correct technique but to make artwork utilizing the tools at hand. While there are skill-based aspects of the course that are important, the rules of printmaking are not meant to hinder creativity. So in that spirit, we will work together in a way that allows you to consider the possibilities of your own interests and how those relate to printmaking.

This is very important:

Do not miss demo days! It is unlikely that the demo will be repeated for your benefit. If you have a valid reason please speak to the instructor before hand and maybe we can work something out. There are so many demos in the semester and a lot of work to be completed during class time that you should carefully consider the ramifications of missing a class.

Terms for Grading

  • Has the student employed the criteria and techniques set forth in the assignment?
  • Was the artwork produced for the assignment created deliberately and thoughtfully?
  • Has the student visually interpreted an idea relating to their interests or the theme of the assignment?
  • Has the student demonstrated an understanding of the basic elements and principles of art?
    • ie. line, shape, color, value, form, texture, space, balance, contrast, emphasis, movement, pattern, rhythm, and variety
  • Has the student produced a consistent print edition where appropriate for the assignment?
  • Were the safety procedures and safe handling of tools and chemicals in the print shop followed correctly by the student?
  • When appropriate has the student participated both in discussions and during class work time on their assignments? Did the student complete the assignment on time and in full?


Sketchbook

Your Sketchbook is a tool for research and personal investigation from your life or imagination. This is one of the most important aspects of this class. Carry your sketchbook constantly! You are establishing the modalities of how you will work as an artist within it. We will look at your sketchbook progress during the semester.
  • Here are some notes on the Sketchbook:
  • It should be large enough to contain your ideas but not so large that it is a hindrance to take with you
  • It will be idea driven, organized and make sense when you go back to review your notes
  • It will contain clear notes on processes introduced in the class, readings, slide lectures, and critiques
  • Keep everything, no tearing out pages or throwing ideas away — they could be useful later
  • Reflect your thinking on assignments, steps, sketches and development of your imagery
  • Show your progress on working within themes and / or your own artistic interests
  • It is not just a scrapbook

Artist’s Statement

As part of this course you will be developing an artist statement in conjunction to your work. At the very minimum it should be one half typed page or equivalent to 250 words. It is not a biography or a CV, it is an opportunity for the artist to establish the context within which the work may be understood.
  • Here are the steps in drafting that statement, and these will be due at various points in the semester.
  • Start by reading the provided text by Nyland Blake’s on artist’s statements
  • Next work on list making, create lists of your interests, answer the questions what? and why?
  • First Draft, will draw from your lists and expand and improve your context
  • Second Draft, will revise the first draft and will have recommendations as well as questions to consider from the instructor
  • Final Draft, will be a succinct statement, this should expand to a full page and be a completely fleshed out statement about your work, taking into consideration what you learned in your critiques

Studio Etiquette & Work time

The assigned projects will require at least 5 to 10 hours and any additional hours of outside class time per week. Printmaking during class time will be limited since there will be so many people working in the shop. Manage your time wisely. You must be present for a demo to gain access to the studios. There will be particular hours that the studio will be available to you after class and on the weekends, with the AT and printshop monitors and you should be proactive with managing your time. There will be no shop time during the semester breaks. You should plan your time accordingly to allow for enough time in the print shop to order materials and complete your projects. Read the syllabus and know what’s happening, come to class prepared to take notes, print your work and participate. If you are not sure about something—please ask! There will be monitors and the AT around to guide you safely and you should

never be printing alone in the studios!


Be aware of deadlines and be ready for critique days. Exhibit a good work ethic, be inquisitive and consistent in your efforts. Clean up as you go since the monitors, AT or instructor are not there to clean up after you and please be thrifty with materials so that there is enough materials for everyone. If you destroy or damage equipment you will be responsible for the monetary value to replace that equipment. Treat the space with the upmost respect.

Statement on Lab Fees

Lab fees for this and other designated courses in the Department of Art, Architecture and Design are set at $100 per student. They provide funding for many of the consumable materials, supplies, and services provided for your use in the course. Most of these are purchased on your behalf at quantity discounts that would be unavailable if purchasing the materials individually. Depending on the class, lab fees may also provide for specialized design software accessible to you in the department’s PC and Mac labs, and through remote use in the virtualization of those computer terminals. Those classes that utilize the university’s Design Labs will have a portion of their fee directed to cover materials and other shop consumables such as blades, abrasives and adhesives. In courses that include site visits to off-campus locations, lab fees may also cover some or all the cost of group travel and transportation. In this course, the majority of the lab fee is budgeted for: materials and supplies including paper, adhesives; tools including rulers, blades, brushes, and applicators The lab fee will provide the minimum required for the successful completion of the course. However, throughout the semester, you may also need to purchase additional materials and supplies on your own. What you decide to purchase and how much you spend will vary depending on the design choices you make. Spending more money may not make for a better project, although sometimes a good idea requires an investment to see it through. More likely, it will require time and effort rather than money. It is recommended that total additional out of pocket expenses for this course be kept between $50 and $75.
